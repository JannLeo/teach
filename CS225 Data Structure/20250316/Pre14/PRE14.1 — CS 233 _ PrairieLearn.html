The Big Picture

In computing, one important performance goal is completing execution of computations faster. In our single-cycle datapath, the speed of computation is limited by the amount of delay in the datapath. The system clock period must be longer than the worst-case path delay in our datapath. Because we can execute only one instruction, this clock period determines the speed of our calculations.

As we will learn later, real processors are more complicated that our single-cycle datapth, able to execute multiple instructions in parallel or taking many clock cycles to execute certain instructions. These complications mean that we must understand two competing performance goals: latency and throughput. Latency is the time it takes to complete one task. Throughput is the number of tasks we can complete in a time unit. For our single-cycle datapath, these two are the same. Usually, improving latency or throughput comes at the cost of making the other metric worse. Because our programs must execute many instructions, we generally want to optimize for throughput when designing processors.

Fun interesting video about the importance of resiliency in computing: Effects of radiation on computing

Our single-cycle datapath is inefficient because only one component is really working at a time during a clock cycle. But if we are clever/careful, we can divide our datapath into several smaller stages that we call a pipeline that allow several components to work in parallel. Creating a pipelined datapath increases throughput by allowing multiple components to act in parallel but at the cost of latency as now faster stages may need to wait for a clock edge that is dictated by slower stages.

Beyond the throughput/latency trade-off, there is one other major trade-off to having a pipelined datapath: we now need to worry about dependencies in our code. In this context, a dependency is any situation where we read data from the register file that was written there by an earlier instruction. Because we are executing parts of different instructions in parallel, it's possible that the calculations happen out of order, creating data hazards. A data hazard is a data dependency that will result in an incorrect calculation. We can use forwarding - passing the conflicting data from a later stage to an earlier stage - to safely resolve some of these data hazards.
